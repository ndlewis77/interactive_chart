Story by Noah Lewis

When looking at the data, most heating complaints came from Central Brooklyn, Northern Manhattan, and the South Bronx. These areas are commonly low income or gentrifying.

A Renthop study shows that heating complaints continue to rise. Over the span of a week over New Year’s last winter a cold snap caused New York city residents to register 21,984 heating complaints.

Last winter’s cold storm some 15,000 people were left without heat in their buildings, causing Governor Andrew Cuomo to consider a state of emergency at the NYC Housing Authority.

What renters should know

During the months of “heat season” – Oct. 1st to March 31st – landlords must keep their buildings heated to 68 degrees from 6 a.m. to 10 p.m. and 62 degrees from 10 p.m. to 6 a.m.

Water temperatures must maintain 120 degrees year round.

Most of the infrastructure in neighborhoods like Flatbush are older and plumbing and electrical units are left to deteriorate from lack of necessary maintenance. The city’s Housing Preservation and Development (HPD) sends inspectors once issues are determined severe enough.

The problems are expensive, and the city ends up footing the bill most of the time. After Cuomo declared a state of emergency, he ordered the appointment of an independent monitor, who would oversee how the NYC Housing Authority would spend it’s $250 million budget to make emergency repairs to the city’s worst infrastructure.
